# BrainLift Writing Guide

This guide trains you to write a BrainLift — a structured research document that proves expertise through depth, not length. It is derived from reviewing and ranking 7+ BrainLifts across multiple domains and identifying what separated the best from the rest.

---

## What a BrainLift Is

A BrainLift is a DOK (Depth of Knowledge) framework document that builds from facts to summaries to insights to spiky points of view. It is not an essay, not a literature review, and not a product spec. It is a research backbone that proves you know a domain well enough to hold an original, defensible position.

The reader should finish it knowing three things:
1. You read the papers and understand what they say
2. You engaged with the evidence that challenges your thesis, not just the evidence that supports it
3. You synthesized something original that no single source says on its own

---

## Document Structure (in order)

### 1. Title
Short, memorable, states the thesis or reframes the problem.

Good examples:
- "When the Muscle Quits"
- "The Piano Practice Gap"
- "The History Gap: Failing Young Learners by Waiting Too Long"
- "What the Camera Can't See"

Bad examples:
- "A Study of EMG Applications in Exercise Science"
- "BrainLift: Research Document"

### 2. Purpose
2-3 sentences maximum. States what the BrainLift proves, names the domain, and identifies the core beliefs the research rests on.

Pattern: "The purpose of this BrainLift is to [build/prove/establish] [what] for [who]. It is built on [core belief 1], [core belief 2], and [core belief 3]."

Rules:
- No hedging. State the beliefs directly.
- The purpose is about the science, not the product. The product is a consequence.
- Keep it tight. If it's more than 3 sentences, cut.

Good example:
"The purpose of this BrainLift is to build a research-backed foundation for a wearable injury-prevention coach for lifters. It starts from three beliefs: that fatigue silently shifts load from the muscles doing the work to the tissues that aren't built for it, that lifters can't feel this shift happening on their own, and that surface EMG paired with computer vision can catch it before injury happens."

### 3. North Star
One question the product or research must answer. Not a mission statement. A testable question.

Good: "Can a garment catch the moment a muscle quits and tell the athlete before their tendons do?"
Bad: "How can we make exercise safer for everyone?"

### 4. In Scope
Each bullet is a promise that the BrainLift goes deep on this topic. Every In Scope item must have corresponding evidence in the Knowledge Tree. If you can't defend it with a source, don't scope it in.

Rules:
- Each bullet should follow logically from the one before (a chain, not a list)
- Include the opposing arguments in scope, don't quarantine them
- 5-8 bullets is the sweet spot
- Format: "[Topic] — [what specifically about it]"

### 5. Out of Scope
What you deliberately chose not to cover and why. This shows the reader you know the adjacent territory and chose boundaries intentionally.

Rules:
- Name what's out and give a short reason
- Don't reference other team members by name if the document should read as standalone
- 4-6 bullets is enough

### 6. DOK 4 — Spiky Points of View (SPOVs)
The pinnacle of the BrainLift. A SPOV is a contrarian, defensible claim synthesized from multiple DOK 3 insights. It is "spiky" because it makes someone in the industry disagree.

**The test: if your teammate reads it and nods along, it's not spiky enough.**

Structure per SPOV:
- **Title:** Short, punchy, reframes the problem. Pattern: take what the industry believes and flip it.
- **Elaboration:** One paragraph. Name competitors or specific approaches. Cite 2-3 sources from your Knowledge Tree. State the counter-argument honestly. Deliver the rebuttal without dodging.

Rules:
- Name names. Competitors, products, researchers.
- Every SPOV must have a named counter-argument and an honest rebuttal baked into the elaboration.
- SPOVs should build on each other in sequence. Each one sets up the next.
- 3-5 SPOVs total. Quality over quantity.
- The counter-argument should be the best version of the opponent's case, not a strawman.

Good SPOV title examples:
- "The fitness industry treats gym fear as a motivation problem. It's an information problem."
- "Every form-check app on the market sees the rep break down. None of them can tell you why."
- "Most school mindfulness companies are selling outcomes the research will not back."
- "Kids don't learn consequences from rules; they learn them from history."

Bad SPOV title examples:
- "EMG is useful for detecting fatigue" (true but not contrarian)
- "Our product is better than competitors" (sales pitch, not a POV)
- "Technology can help athletes" (obvious, no one disagrees)

### 7. Experts
5-8 experts. Mix of researchers and practitioners if possible.

Per expert:
- **Who:** Name and title
- **Focus:** Primary area of expertise in 1-2 sentences
- **Why Follow:** How this specific expert's work connects to a specific claim in YOUR BrainLift. Not generic relevance. Specific dependency.
- **Where:** Link to their work

Rule: If an expert is in your list, you should be able to explain in conversation why their work matters to your specific argument. If you can't, cut them.

### 8. DOK 3 — Insights
Insights are original conclusions drawn from combining 2+ DOK 2 sources. Each insight must state something that no single source says on its own.

Structure per insight:
- **Theme label** (e.g., "The Fear-Danger Gap," "The Invisible Layer")
- **Body:** Name the sources, state what each says individually, then state what they say together that's new. One paragraph.

Rules:
- Every insight must name specific sources with years
- The synthesis must be non-obvious. If Source A says X and the insight just restates X, it's not an insight.
- 3-5 insights total
- Each insight should connect to at least one SPOV

Good insight pattern:
"[Source A] found [X]. [Source B] found [Y]. Neither paper talks to the other. Together they say something neither says alone: [Z]."

Bad insight pattern:
"Research shows that [general claim]." (No sources named, no synthesis, just a summary)

### 9. DOK 2 — Knowledge Tree
The evidence foundation. Organized into categories with subcategories tagged as Supporting, Challenging, or Emerging.

**Category structure:**
- 3-5 categories
- Each category should have at least one Supporting and one Challenging subcategory
- Emerging subcategories are for evidence that points toward your thesis but hasn't been tested in your specific context

**Per subcategory:**
- **Source:** Full citation
- **DOK 1 — Facts:** 1-3 specific, citable facts from the paper. Not interpretations. What the paper actually found.
- **DOK 2 — Summary:** In your own voice, what this paper means for your argument. Written as if you're explaining it to a smart person over coffee. No academic tone. No hedging.
- **Link:** DOI or URL

Rules for DOK 1 facts:
- Each fact should be distinct. Don't say the same thing three ways.
- If you only have one fact, that's fine. Don't pad.
- Facts are what the paper found. Summaries are what it means.

Rules for DOK 2 summaries:
- Write in your voice. Short sentences. No hedging. No em dashes if your style avoids them.
- The summary should not restate the facts. It should interpret them for your argument.
- Challenging summaries should end on the challenge, not pivot to your rebuttal. Save rebuttals for insights and SPOVs.
- Keep summaries to 2-4 sentences.

**Source overlap is fine.** If one paper serves two categories, use it in both with different DOK 1 facts and different DOK 2 summaries for each appearance. This shows deeper engagement, not laziness.

### 10. Key Assumptions (optional but strong)
List the assumptions your thesis depends on, each with an explicit failure condition.

Format: "[Assumption]. Fail: [what would prove it wrong]."

Example:
- "Sensor tolerance: athletes wear the sensor through a full session. Fail: more than 30% remove it early."
- "Signal quality: MyoWare envelope holds through sweat and movement. Fail: mid-workout signal degradation."

### 11. Self-Critique (optional but strong)
A checklist of what the BrainLift does and doesn't cover, with honest gaps flagged.

Format:
- [x] Every SPOV has a named counter and rebuttal
- [x] Every insight traces to DOK 2 sources
- [ ] Gap: [honest gap you haven't addressed yet]

## Writing Voice Rules

- Short sentences. No hedging.
- No em dashes unless the author's style uses them.
- No "it is important to note" or "it should be mentioned that."
- Summaries sound like you're talking to a smart person, not writing an abstract.
- Humanize everything. If a sentence sounds like it came from an AI, rewrite it.
- Challenging evidence should feel like real opposition, not a token acknowledgment you dismiss immediately.
- When you name a counter-argument, state the strongest version of it, not a strawman.

## Quality Checks Before Finishing

1. **The interview test:** If someone points at any source and asks "what does this paper say," can the author answer without looking at it? If not, either the author needs to read the abstract or the source should be cut.
2. **The teammate test:** If a teammate reads a SPOV and immediately nods along, it's not spiky enough. Rewrite it.
3. **The "so what" test:** Every In Scope bullet must connect to the Knowledge Tree. Every insight must connect to a SPOV. If any section is orphaned, cut it or connect it.
4. **The depth test:** Fewer strong sources you know cold beats many sources you can't discuss. 9-15 unique sources is the sweet spot. 20+ means you probably can't defend them all.
5. **The standalone test:** Can someone outside your team read this and understand the argument without knowing your project? If not, remove internal references and team-specific context.

## What Separates the Top-Ranked BrainLifts

Based on reviewing and ranking 7+ BrainLifts:

1. **Opposing evidence integrated throughout, not quarantined in one section.** The best BrainLifts have challenging sources in every Knowledge Tree category, not a separate "Opposing Arguments" section at the end.

2. **SPOVs that name competitors and fight the strongest counter.** Generic claims ("technology can help") rank low. Specific claims that name who disagrees and why rank high.

3. **Insights that synthesize, not summarize.** The best insights state what multiple sources say together that none says alone. The worst restate a single source's finding as if it's an insight.

4. **Self-critique and honest gaps.** The top-ranked BrainLifts explicitly state where they might be wrong and what would prove it. This builds credibility, it doesn't undermine the argument.

5. **Every expert maps to a specific claim.** Generic "this person is important in the field" entries rank low. "This person's work is why we claim X" entries rank high.

6. **The author's voice is present.** The best BrainLifts sound like a person wrote them, not a literature review generator. Short sentences, direct claims, human language.
